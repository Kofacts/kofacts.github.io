notes einstein

May 2026 · 3 min

finally finished einstein.

the popular image of einstein is that of a man who arrived at a few revolutionary discoveries and spent the rest of his life basking in them. the book paints a different picture: a restless mind that seemed incapable of leaving a problem alone once it sensed an inconsistency beneath the surface.

i think the more interesting thing about einstein is that he has become the cultural frontispiece for genius itself, when the reality appears far less mystical. he was, above all, relentlessly curious. he seemed incapable of living comfortably with confusion. if something did not make sense to him, he pursued it with an almost unreasonable persistence until it yielded an answer. that trait feels increasingly quaint in an age where information is abundant but genuine curiosity is scarce.

the details of his personal life were often as fascinating as the science itself. there are moments in the book that leave you genuinely perplexed - his relationships, his treatment of those closest to him, and, of course, the curious fact that he eventually married elsa, his cousin. the result is a portrait that feels less like a saint of science and more like a brilliant, flawed, and often contradictory human being.

a question i’ve found myself returning to lately is one of intent. what is the thing a person is ultimately aiming at?

for einstein, i don’t think it was fame, recognition, or even discovery for its own sake. it seemed to be coherence. he possessed an almost stubborn conviction that reality ought to make sense, that beneath the apparent disorder of the world there existed principles that fit together cleanly and elegantly. whether he was studying light, gravity, space, or time, he seemed animated by the belief that there was a deeper order waiting to be uncovered.

that conviction produced some of the most important insights in modern science. it also became the source of his greatest frustrations. as physics moved toward probability, uncertainty, and quantum mechanics, einstein remained unwilling to abandon the belief that nature was, at some deeper level, intelligible and unified.

another thing that struck me was how effectively he occupied the public imagination. when people think of genius, they think of einstein, not bohr, schrödinger, or tesla. their contributions were no less profound, yet einstein became the symbol. part of that was undoubtedly intellect, but part of it was something else: timing, personality, visibility, and an intuitive understanding of how to make himself legible to the wider world.

the book reminded me of a lesson that extends far beyond science. the people who win are rarely the most acute minds in the room. acuity is only one variable among many. persistence, narrative, timing, and the ability to make others care about your work often matter just as much.

more than a biography of a physicist, this felt like a study of obsession - of what happens when a person commits himself entirely to a single way of seeing the world. whether einstein was ultimately right about the universe is a question for physicists. what stayed with me was something simpler: the realization that some of history’s greatest achievements begin not with intelligence, but with an unwillingness to stop asking questions.
